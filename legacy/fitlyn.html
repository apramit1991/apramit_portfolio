Designing an Action-First System for Gym Operations

How I designed an action-first platform to simplify dues, renewals, communication, and reporting — using real user insights and AI-assisted workflows.

Why Fitlyn Exists

Indian gyms were still running daily operations across registers, Excel, WhatsApp, and memory

Fitlyn started from a practical operating gap. Memberships, dues, renewals, communication, and reporting were spread across disconnected tools, making routine work harder to track and easier to miss.

The Core Problem

The real issue was not just missing software - it was not knowing what needed action today

The most important work was revenue-critical and time-sensitive: dues, renewals, reminders, payments, and reporting visibility. When those signals were scattered, gym owners had to reconstruct priorities from memory and chats instead of seeing them clearly in one place.

What was breaking down

Operators had to remember who was due, who needed a reminder, which members had dropped off, and whether reporting matched actual business activity. The product challenge was not just visual polish. It was about making recurring operational work easier to see, prioritize, and execute.

Fitlyn was designed to reduce manual gym operations and create a more reliable system for renewals, dues, communication, and reporting.

User Insight

Action mattered more than analytics

Once the operating problem became clear, one insight kept repeating in conversations and walkthroughs. This became the filter for every product decision that followed.

Observed in operator conversations

  • Teams relied on memory for dues and renewals.
  • Follow-ups were mostly done through WhatsApp threads.
  • Excel sheets and physical registers were still common.

What that meant for product direction

  • Dues and renewals needed to be visible from the first screen.
  • Communication needed to feel structured, not ad hoc.
  • Analytics had to support action, not dominate the workflow.

Turning Point

The clearest signal from user conversations

"I just need to know who has not paid today - everything else is secondary."

What this changed in the product direction

  • Dues visibility was prioritized ahead of deeper analytics.
  • The dashboard became action-first, not analysis-first.
  • Reminders and renewals were elevated into primary workflows.

Design Direction

From exploration to clearer product decisions

Once the operating problem became clear, the design focus shifted from reporting depth to daily action. Early explorations helped surface multiple possibilities, but the final direction was shaped by what gym owners actually needed to act on daily.

Iteration 1: Feature-rich dashboard vs action-first visibility

Initial directionThe early dashboard direction included several widgets and charts, including operational metrics like WhatsApp/SMS messages sent, multiple analytics cards, and additional status widgets.

ProblemWhile the screen looked comprehensive, it created unnecessary complexity for non-technical gym owners. Metrics like daily WhatsApp/SMS count were not important to them during routine operations. They cared more about what needed action: pending dues, expiring memberships, recent payments, and follow-ups.

Final decisionI reduced the dashboard to focus only on actionable, high-priority information that gym owners wanted to see daily.

OutcomeThe dashboard became easier to scan, less overwhelming, and more aligned with real gym operations. Instead of showing everything the system could track, it focused on what owners needed to act on.

Iteration 2: Navigation Structure

Initial directionFeature-based module grouping.

ProblemDid not match the user mental model for daily gym work.

Final decisionShifted to task-based navigation tied to operator routines.

OutcomeQuicker access to dues, renewals, attendance, and reminders.

Initial directionAI-generated multiple layouts and feature directions quickly.

Final decisionUsed AI for expansion and speed, then filtered with business logic and usability.

Exploration Directions

Testing how much information the product should surface, and where

These explorations helped compare reporting depth, action visibility, and overall cognitive load before the final UI direction was locked.

Direction A: Metric-heavy dashboard

Useful for visibility, but too dense for fast action. It risked overwhelming owners with numbers before surfacing what needed attention first.

Direction B: Action-first hierarchy

This direction created a better balance between operational priorities and business metrics. It reduced cognitive load while keeping revenue-critical actions visible.

Direction C: Minimal admin shell

Cleaner at first glance, but too shallow for a product that needs quick access to reporting, attendance, and messaging workflows in the same ecosystem.

What improved

Action queues were made more visible so dues and renewals could not be missed easily.

Why it mattered

Business metrics stayed visible, but they no longer competed with the primary daily tasks.

Design lens

The final direction was tuned for operators who need confidence and speed, not product training.

Reflections

What this project clarified about AI-assisted product design and human judgment

Fitlyn reinforced that AI can accelerate exploration, but product direction still depends on user context, business priority, and judgment.

Where AI helped most

AI made early expansion faster. It was especially useful for surfacing feature possibilities, module structures, and multiple visual directions without slowing the process down.

Where human judgment stayed critical

Prioritization, usability tradeoffs, and business practicality still depended on product judgment. The strongest direction was the one that felt most usable for the real operational context, not the one that looked most elaborate.

How I would evolve Fitlyn next

I would deepen the communication layer, add smarter reporting views, and build channel-level workflows under messaging so reminders and retention campaigns become more systematic.
